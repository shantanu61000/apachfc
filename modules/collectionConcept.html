Collection Concept

DPD Calculation:

If Keith makes payment toward his January EMI anytime during the month, his loan account will move back to 0 DPD

But if he doesn't:
On 31st January, his loan account shall be 27 DPD
On 4th February, his loan account would be 31 DPD

At this time, the Feb EMI is also due.
If he bounces the Feb EMI the DPD continues to increase by 1 each day.

However, if he clears the Feb EMI:
it shall go towards setting off the January emi

Why ..?

Because: The rule is, any payment received is first updated towards the earliest EMI.

When the Feb EMI clears, and it gets adjusted towards January dues, what effectively remains due now is the February EMI

Now, if you have to calculate the DPD as on 7th Feb         

Overdues:

'Overdues' are the amounts due but not paid. In other words, overdue are sum of all the EMIs that have d=fallen due till now but unpaid

The sum of inerest portion in these EMIs would be the overdue interest.

The sum of principal portion in these EMIs would be overdue principal

NPA and Provision

NPA:

Loan amounts are "Assets" for a company because the generate income in the form of interest

However, when a customer defaults their EMI, htere us no interest earned. Not only that, their principal amount is also at a high risk of default.

When such an account remains unpaid for more than 90 days (i.e More than 90 DPD), it is called a "Non Performing Asset"

Provision for Loss:

We are required to make a "provision for loss" on every account that is classified ad NPA.
A Provision is made by setting aside a portion from the profits.

We Know that:
Profit = Income - Expenses

If a provision has to be made, then:
Profit = Income - Expenses - Provision for loss

Thus, provision for loss has the effect of reducing our profits

The amount of provision is decided by the NPA category to which the loan is classified. For NPAs that have DPDs, a higher percentage is set aside as a provision.

Hard Bucket

Mid Range Collection ( > 60 DPD):
More focus of intensive collection efforts need to be carried out at this collection stage of delinquency
Where collection is not successful collection manager should initiate section 138 case with the help of legal team post approval from Collection head

The aim of the collection workforce is to ensure that the account is retained as current by collecting all the overdue payments along with late payment charges, etc.

Skip Tracing Process

         "Skip" is a case where both the asset and/or the customer are missing. Where an account enters into collections, some accounts may not have updated customer information that in turn may lead to communication breakdown. Collection should make efforts internally on the system to correct and update information.

Skip may be:

Unintentional Skips: Borrowers who have inadvertently failed to notify APAC about a change of address,
Or
Intentional Skips: Borrowers who purposefully move and avoid APAC representatives or officers.

Once a borrower is traced, the collection staff must verify all account information for and ensure correctness that necessary changes are made on MIFIN as per established APAC procedures.

DO's and DON'Ts

        In today’s tough economy, customers may be having more trouble than normal paying their debts. As in every industry, there are good ways and bad ways to perform debt collections. Here are some ways to increase your success.

1. Be Prepared:

You make the initial contact with a delinquent customer, make sure you know everything you can about the customer. Make copies of Loan Documents and any other information that will help you speak knowledgeably, professionally and personally with the customer.

2. Document Everything:

While talking to the customer about the outstanding debt, take careful notes about everything that was discussed, including the customer’s comments. Input everything into the Notes, while the conversation is fresh in your mind. Over time, continue to add any additional details to your file to keep it as up-to-date as possible.

3. Don’t Assume Anything:

When making your initial debt collection call, quickly make sure that the debt has in fact not been paid. Don’t alienate the customer. Remember there may be potential future business with the customer. The debt in question could be a mistake and not a collection problem at all. Be careful with your tone and your words at this point. Wait and listen to what the customer has to say and be sure to document the interaction carefully and accurately.

4. Be Pleasant and Control Yourself:

The tone you take with your voice can impact how successful the conversation will be. If you start off the conversation with a friendly, non-confrontational tone, the customer may respond more positively.. If you act like you care or can understand the debtor’s side of the story, you may be able to prevent the person from becoming defensive.

4. Avoid Confrontation and Manipulation:

Attempt to find out if the debtor’s excuse for not paying is legitimate. For example, if the person blames the non-payment on someone else, confirm this is true or false by contacting the other person. Listen carefully to what the person is telling you and get a sense of whether the person is being honest with you. Often, your gut feeling will be right on.

5. Put a Stop to Anger or Harassment:

Always try to stay calm even if the debtor becomes abusive during the contact. If this happens, you might suggest calling back later. No matter what, always listen carefully to what the customer tells you and try to keep the dialogue constructive as much as you can.

6. Keep Communicating:

Even if the debtor can’t pay right away, it is always important to keep communications going. He may be able to pay in the future, and by talking to the debtor and really listening to what he has to say, you may be able to help him figure out a way to start paying sooner
