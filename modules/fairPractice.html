Fair Practice Code

How must one read the FPC
  • Read the clause
  • Imagine a business situation
  • Determine application of the clause in that situation
Example 1:

Access the Fair practices code on our website under the head “services”:

Read Clause no. 4.3 on confidentiality of customer information

Now imagine: A sales agency approaches us and proposes to enter into an arrangements to give us a regular fee in exchange of the name and contact details of our customers.

If we agree to the arrangement, it would be a clear violation of this clause.

Exercise 1: Read a few other clauses and determine their applicability

The scope of FPC is not fixed. There might be instances which might in essence be an “unfair practice” though it is not directly in the codes.

Developing the ability to judge and specifically distinguish what constitutes “Unfair” in a day to day business dealings is the supreme importance.

Some examples of “Unfair practices”:
  • Forging a customer’s signature : unfair on the customer
  • Forging a builder’s signature : unfair on the builder and thus indirectly unfair on the customer too
    (Forging is also a criminal offence in low)
  • Making a customer sign incomplete documents (unfilled application form/loan agreements/ ECS form, etc) : Customer signs a document without knowing all the details – unfair on customer
  • Mixing customer’s cash with personal cash : unfair on customer
  • Misstatement of information about a customer during credit appraisal :apart from risking the portfolio quality, it also give a specific advantage to one customer and therefore unfair on other customers

A seemingly routine process in your location might raise a question in your mind about its fairness. It is our responsibility to analyse such process until we satisfy that it meets the test of fairness. If not, we must escalate the matter appropriately

Th process of escalation is outlined in what is called the “The Whistler Blower Policy” . The policy is detailed under the heading “Services” on our website.
